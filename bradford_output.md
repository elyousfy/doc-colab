**Hydrogen Production Package Part A**

The core component of the hydrogen generation system is the BEHS high efficiency alkaline electrolyser, used to produce hydrogen and oxygen through the process of electrolysis of water. To ensure that the produced hydrogen meets the project requirements, the system is equipped with a lye gas separation and purification system to remove water, alkaline solution, and trace gases such as oxygen from the hydrogen product, ensuring the purity of the hydrogen. The system is supported by various utility systems such as the water purification system that purifies tap water into demineralized water ready to be mixed with a dosage of Hydrogen Peroxide flake. The system also integrates an Energy Management System (EMS), enabling the electrolyser to adapt to various power sources such as the dynamic load fluctuations generated during photovoltaic (PV) power generation. The EMS can adjust the electrolyser load in real-time based on the power fluctuations from the photovoltaic field output, ensuring that the hydrogen production process meets project requirements even under unstable green electricity supply conditions.

To ensure the stable operation of the hydrogen production system, the project is equipped with a comprehensive set of utilities systems, including a purified water system, cooling system, power supply system, instrument air supply system, and control and monitoring systems. The purified water system supplies high-purity water to the electrolyser, and it includes a purified water unit, water storage tanks, and purified water pumps to ensure the purity and stable supply of water during the hydrogen production process. The power supply system consists of switching equipment, rectifiers, and rectifier transformers, ensuring a stable power supply for the system's operation. The cooling water and chilled water circulation systems, combined with heat exchangers, are used to control the temperature of the system, ensuring the equipment operates under optimal conditions. Other utilities facilities include pipelines and connecting devices, control systems, lighting, CCTV systems, lightning protection and grounding systems, operational pathways, wastewater collection pond, nitrogen cylinder racks, and instrument air supply systems. These systems collectively ensure the stable operation and safety of the hydrogen production system.

In terms of safety, the hydrogen production system is equipped with hydrogen analysers, hydrogen leak detectors, and flame detectors. These devices can trigger emergency shutdowns in the event of a hydrogen leak or other emergency situations, preventing any potential hazards to the plant. An independent Safety Instrumented System (SIS) is responsible for the safety monitoring of the hydrogen production system, ensuring automatic safety measures are taken in emergency situations. In addition, the system is equipped with real-time monitoring and data logging software and hardware, enabling continuous monitoring and recording of system operation, ensuring comprehensive data collection. The system also includes an alkaline solution conductivity detector to promptly identify anomalies in the electrolyser operation, thereby enhancing the operational stability of the system. Furthermore, the electrolyser cells voltage monitoring equipment improves the precision of monitoring the electrolyser operating conditions. Independent alkaline supply and recovery systems are provided to facilitate the handling of alkaline solution during commissioning startup and shutdown phases. The alkaline solution circulation pumps and makeup water pumps are configured in a one-operating, one-standby mode, effectively reducing the impact of equipment failures on system operation.

The hydrogen production system containers and equipment are equipped with insulation to maintain the temperature of the alkaline solution for extended periods, minimizing restart time and energy consumption. This insulation also ensures safe and stable operation under various conditions. The equipment is supplied in a containerized format, with a high level of integration and a compact layout, effectively saving space and reducing civil construction costs. The system is also designed with external protective features such as dust, hail, and wind resistance to withstand the harsh desert conditions. Additionally, an air-conditioned control room is provided to ensure the equipment operates smoothly in extreme environments while offering a comfortable workspace for operators.

The hydrogen produced by the BEHS electrolyser is routed into our fully integrated

**Storage &amp; Compression Package Part B and Part C** , which comprises:

**Multi-stage Compression** : Low-pressure hydrogen is first pressurized by eight (8) 45 MPa, oil-free, hydraulically driven compressors, each rated at 1 000 Nm³/h @ 1.4 MPa inlet. A second stage of two (2) 70 MPa compressors further boosts pressure for high-pressure dispensing and cylinder filling. All compressors feature real-time leak and oil-contamination monitoring, and are supported by dedicated air-cooled chillers to maintain discharge temperatures ≤ 40 °C.

**Buffering &amp; Storage** : To smooth transient flows and decouple production from dispensing, the system includes either seven (7) × 5 m³ or two (2) × 20 m³ buffer tanks at 1.4 MPa. #### 2.1.7 Process Technology Introduction

Hydrogen production by water electrolysis is a process of decomposing electrolyte dissolved in water into new substances by means of direct current. However, when direct current is introduced into some electrolyte aqueous solutions, the decomposed substances have nothing associated with the original electrolyte. What is decomposed is pure water as the solvent, and the original electrolyte, such as sulfuric acid, sodium hydroxide and potassium hydroxide, remains in the water.

In the electrolysis of water, as pure water is a typical weak electrolyte because of its low ionization and low conductivity, it is necessary to add the aforementioned electrolytes to increase the conductivity of the solution, so that pure water can be electrolysed into hydrogen and oxygen.

In the past, sodium hydroxide was often used as electrolyte in China, but as the material of electrolysis stack can resist the corrosion of potassium hydroxide, potassium hydroxide solution has been commonly used as electrolyte to save electric energy. Electrolytes such as potassium hydroxide will not be electrolysed. Taking potassium hydroxide as an example, it is a strong electrolyte, and when dissolved in water, the ionization process occurs as follows:

KOH &lt;=&gt; K+ + OH-

Therefore, a large amount of K+ and OH- are produced in aqueous solution. Metal ions have different activity in aqueous solution, and have a decreasing activity as follows: K&gt;Na&gt;Mg&gt;Al&gt;Mn&gt;Zn&gt;Fe&gt;Ni&gt;Sn&gt;Pb&gt;H&gt;Cu&gt;Hg&gt;Ag&gt;Au

Pure water is a weak electrolyte and is difficult to electrolyze. However, when KOH is dissolved in water, polar water molecules surround ionized K+ and become hydrated potassium ions, and the water molecules get polar orientations due to the action of K+. Under the action of direct current, K+ moves to the cathode together with the water molecules with polar orientations, and then H+ will get electrons first and become hydrogen. When the electrolytic equation of water acts on potassium hydroxide aqueous solution under the action of direct current, the following discharge reactions occur at the cathode and anode, as illustrated below:

<!-- image -->

The process of water electrolysis into hydrogen and oxygen and its electrode reactions are as follows:

Cathode: 4H 2 O+4e=2H 2 ↑+4OH-

The H+ (produced by ionization of water) in the electrolyte is attracted by and moves to the cathode where it receives electrons to form hydrogen.

Anode: 4OH- = 2H 2 O+O 2 ↑+4e

The OH- in the electrolyte is attracted by and moves to the anode and finally releases electrons to form water and oxygen.

Total reaction: 2H 2 O=2H 2 ↑+ O 2 ↑

In the electrolysis process where KOH is the electrolyte, water is actually electrolyzed to produce hydrogen and oxygen, while KOH only plays the role of carrying charge.

The theoretical water consumption for producing 1m 3 hydrogen is 0.804kg. The actual water consumption is higher than the theoretical one because both hydrogen and oxygen carry away some water.

As a result, the actual water consumption for producing 1m 3 of hydrogen is about 0.85kg to 0.9kg.

A water electrolysis cell is composed of a pair of electrodes immersed in the electrolyte and a diaphragm spaced between them to prevent gas penetration. Water will be electrolyzed by direct current at a certain voltage (above the decomposition voltage of water, 1.23V, and the thermal balance voltage, 1.47V). According to the output demand of user, several groups of electrolysis cells are combined to reduce the volume and increase the output, thus forming a pressure filtration combined structure of electrolysis stack. Generally, a medium-pressure electrolysis stack adopts a parallel structure of the left and right stacks, with the middle pole plate connected to positive DC power supply and both end pole plates connected to negative DC power supply. It uses double polarity pole plates and diaphragm spacers to form multiple electrolysis cells, and it has common inlet and outlet in the lower part of the stack, and respective gas and liquid channels for hydrogen and oxygen bases in the upper part. A tandem-type structure with one positive and two negatives or one positive and one negative is used, and its internal structure is similar to the coupled-type structure.

Control System (includes EMS)

Three-Layer Control System Architecture Overview:

<!-- image -->

Each layer has distinct roles and responsibilities, as described below:

1. Supervisory Layer:

This layer consists of the Upper Computer, which is responsible for system-wide monitoring and data visualization. It provides operators with real-time operational status, historical data, and alarm information. The Supervisory Layer does not perform direct control actions but serves as the interface for system monitoring and information display.

1. Control Layer:

The Control Layer is the core of the system operational control. It primarily utilizes the Energy Management System (EMS) and Programmable Logic Controllers (PLCs) to execute control commands and manage equipment operations. The structure is as follows:

- EMS (Energy Management System): The EMS collects real-time data from the system, optimizes system operation, and issues control commands. It ensures the balance between photovoltaic power output and hydrogen production load.
- Process PLC: The Process PLC receives control commands from the EMS and regulates the hydrogen production load by controlling the PCS Cabinet (Power Conversion System), which adjusts the operational status of the electrolyser.
- Safety PLC: The Safety PLC is responsible for safety protection functions, ensuring safe operation of the system. It works with the SIS Cabinet (Safety Instrumented System) to monitor and control critical system parameters, preventing hazardous conditions.
2. Field Layer:

The Field Layer comprises physical equipment, including photovoltaic generation units, electrolysers, and other hydrogen production devices. This layer is responsible for executing actual operations and communicates with the control system through sensors and actuators. It provides real-time feedback on equipment status (e.g., temperature, pressure) and performs tasks based on commands from the PLCs.

Safety Instrumented System (SIS) Overview and Design Principles

1. System Overview

The Safety Instrumented System (SIS) is an independent safety protection system separate from the Process Control System (PCS) that reduces risks in production processes. It monitors critical process parameters and takes immediate action, such as triggering emergency shutdowns or activating safety devices, when parameters exceed pre-set safety limits. This helps prevent accidents or mitigate their consequences.

1. Design Principles
2. Independence: The SIS should operate independently from the Basic Process Control System (BPCS) to ensure that even in the event of a BPCS failure, the SIS can reliably execute its safety functions.
3. Reliability: The system should employ highly reliable hardware and software components to ensure accurate and timely responses when needed.
4. Safety: The design and operation of the system must comply with relevant safety standards and regulations to meet all necessary safety requirements.
5. Maintainability: The system should be easy to maintain and test, allowing potential faults to be identified and repaired promptly.
2. System Components
7. Sensors: Sensors monitor key process parameters such as temperature, pressure, flow, and liquid levels. These sensors must be highly reliable and accurate.
8. Logic Controllers: Based on input signals from the sensors, logic controllers make decisions according to pre-defined logic, determining whether safety actions need to be triggered.
9. Actuators: These execute the necessary safety actions based on signals from the logic controllers, such as closing valves, stopping motors, or activating safety devices. The actuators must provide quick and reliable response times.
3. Functional Description:
11. Monitoring Function:
12. Real-time Data Collection: The SIS is equipped with high-precision sensors that continuously and in real-time collect critical process data, including but not limited to temperature, pressure, flow, liquid levels, and concentrations. The sensors transmit the collected data to the logic controllers as electrical signals.
13. Data Processing and Analysis: The logic controllers process and analyze the sensor data. They can perform tasks such as filtering, amplifying, and converting data to improve accuracy and reliability. Additionally, the controllers can analyze the data using pre-set algorithms to assess whether the process is in a normal state.
14. Anomaly Detection and Alarming: When monitored parameters exceed the pre-set safety limits, the SIS immediately issues an alarm. This can be communicated through visual and audible signals, as well as on-screen prompts to alert operators. Additionally, alarm information can be sent to relevant management departments or emergency response centers to facilitate timely action.
15. Logic Decision-Making Function:
16. Safety Logic Design: Safety logic is designed based on the characteristics of the process and safety requirements. Typically, Boolean logic, sequential logic, or other forms of logic are used to determine whether safety risks exist. For example, if both the temperature and pressure exceed certain thresholds, a safety action may be triggered.
17. Logical Operations and Decision-Making: The logic controllers perform logical operations (such as AND, OR, NOT) on the collected data based on the safety logic to determine whether a safety action needs to be triggered. During decision-making, the system can also take into account other factors, such as equipment status or operational mode.
18. Safety Action Execution Function:
19. Actuator Control: When logic determines that a safety action is required, the SIS immediately activates the actuators to carry out the corresponding safety action. Actuators can include valves, motors, pumps, and other equipment that are controlled via electrical, pneumatic, or hydraulic signals.
20. Types of Safety Actions: The type of safety actions depends on the process and safety requirements. Common safety actions include emergency shutdowns, closing valves, and cutting off power supplies. These actions can effectively prevent or mitigate accidents.
21. Response Time: SIS safety actions must have a rapid response time to ensure that preventative measures are taken before an accident occurs. The response time is typically required to be within milliseconds to tens of milliseconds, depending on the process hazards and safety requirements.
22. Fault Diagnosis Function:
23. System Self-Diagnosis: The SIS has self-diagnosis capabilities, enabling it to continuously monitor its own hardware and software. It can detect sensor failures, logic controller faults, actuator malfunctions, etc., and issue warnings when necessary. The system also records fault information for subsequent analysis and troubleshooting.
24. Fault Isolation and Recovery: When a fault is detected, the SIS can automatically isolate the faulty section to ensure the normal operation of other parts of the system. The system can also attempt to automatically restore the faulty section or prompt operators to manually restore it. During fault recovery, the system ensures that safety functions remain unaffected.
25. Remote Diagnosis and Maintenance: To improve maintainability, the SIS can support remote diagnostics and maintenance. Through network connections, maintenance personnel can remotely monitor the system status, diagnose faults, and perform software updates or parameter adjustments, significantly reducing maintenance costs and downtime.
26. Fault History and Analysis: The SIS records a detailed history of all faults, including the time, type, location, and corrective actions taken. This information provides valuable insights for future fault analysis and troubleshooting. Analyzing historical fault data allows maintenance personnel to identify potential issues or weak points in the system, enabling preventive measures to improve reliability. Furthermore, fault history can be used to evaluate system performance and maintenance effectiveness, providing data to support system optimization and improvement.

#### 2.1.9 Compressors

<!-- image -->

The compressor package converts low‐pressure hydrogen from the electrolyser into high‐pressure gas suitable for storage and dispensing. It comprises:

- Multi-stage Hydraulic-Drive Compressors
Eight (8) 45 MPa compressors (1 000 Nm³/h @ 1.4 MPa inlet) and two (2) 70 MPa booster compressors (668 Nm³/h @ 30–50 MPa inlet) are skid-mounted with integrated hydraulic power units and piston-seal cylinders. The oil-free design eliminates gas contamination, and a special packing arrangement prevents hydraulic-oil ingress into the hydrogen stream. Real-time gas-and-oil leak detection ensures purity and safety .

- Containerization &amp; Noise Control
Each compressor train is housed in a weatherproof, insulated container (IP55) with vibration isolation mounts and acoustic enclosures. Noise emissions are limited to &lt; 80 dB(A) at 1 m in compliance with BLCH PSR1 Basic of Design requirements

- Cooling &amp; Thermal Management
Dedicated air-cooled chillers maintain discharge temperatures ≤ 40 °C for 45 MPa compressors and 5–15 °C for 70 MPa compressors. All chiller units feature stainless-steel coolant tanks, closed-loop circuits, and operate on R410a refrigerant

- Control &amp; Instrumentation
Local PLC cabinets with 10″ HMI panels provide compressor start/stop, load sequencing, and safety interlocks. Key process variables (pressure, temperature, vibration, oil level) are monitored continuously, and emergency shutdowns are initiated on alarm conditions. The compressor control system integrates with the central EMS for coordinated plant operation

- Standards &amp; Testing
All compressors conform to ATEX 2014/34/EU, Pressure Equipment (Safety) Regulations 2016, and relevant ISO/EN standards. Factory Acceptance Tests (FAT) and Site Acceptance Tests (SAT) will follow BLCH-SPEC-COM-009 “Compressor Schedule 3 – Performance Guarantees and Testing,” ensuring guaranteed performance levels without manual overrides or bypass of safety interlocks

To provide short‐term buffering and ensure uninterrupted refuelling operations—even under variable production or dispensing rates—we have proposed the storage system to incorporate high‐pressure cylinder banks designed for modular, skid‐mounted installation:

Bulk Buffering &amp; High‐Pressure Reserve

- Nine 45 MPa cylinder sets (6 m³ water volume each) deliver ~1 559 kg of hydrogen capacity, smoothing the difference between electrolyser output and dispenser demand.
- One 90 MPa cylinder set (2 m³ water volume) serves as a high‐pressure reserve, enabling rapid fills and maintaining dispenser pressure during peak demand.

Skid &amp; Piping Design

- Cylinder banks are mounted on galvanized‐steel skids with integrated manifolds, quick‐disconnects, and stainless‐steel (316L) piping per ASME B31.3.
- Each manifold includes fill/discharge valves, back‐pressure regulators, and pressure relief devices (PRVs and bursting discs) to safeguard against overpressure.

Instrumentation &amp; Control Integration

- Pressure transducers and thermocouples monitor each set’s status in real time; signals feed into the central SCADA/PLC for automated refill sequencing, leak detection, and emergency shutdown.
- A local HMI panel on the skid allows manual intervention and diagnostics during commissioning or maintenance.

Safety &amp; Venting

- Overpressure venting is routed through a flame‐arrested DN40 vent line to a safe outdoor location.
- Manual and automatic nitrogen‐purge connections permit inerting of the manifold during cylinder exchange or maintenance.

Environmental &amp; Civil Considerations

- Skids are rated for outdoor use (IP55), with weatherproof coatings, seismic anchoring provisions, and insulation jackets to limit thermal losses in temperature extremes (–40 / +60 °C).
- The compact footprint of the cylinder skids minimizes civil works and simplifies site layout, enabling rapid “plug‐and‐play” installation alongside the compressor and dispenser modules.

#### 2.1.11 Dispenser

<!-- image -->

<!-- image -->

- The dispenser package provides the final link between high-pressure storage and vehicle refuelling. It is designed for fast, accurate, and safe delivery of hydrogen at both 35 MPa and 70 MPa, with fully integrated temperature control, metering, and safety interlocks.

Dispensing Modules

- 35Mpa / 70 MPa Dispensers (TK16/17 Nozzles): Two skid-mounted units, each capable of delivering up to 3.6 kg/min with integrated alcohol pre-cooling to –40 °C. Nozzle supply power 0.3 kW.
- 35 MPa Filling Columns: Eight fixed-column dispensers with cascade pressure control, flowmeters (±1 % accuracy), and automatic residual-pressure compensation to maximize fill speed, one filling column is dual purpose and can serve the vehicle dispenser.

Thermal Conditioning

- Cryogenic pre-cooling loop (R-134a) reduces gas temperature to –40 °C before dispensing, preventing adiabatic heating and ensuring correct density measurement.
- Dedicated chiller units (700 bar service) maintain dispenser and nozzle temperatures within –45 to –40 °C.

Control &amp; Instrumentation

- Local PLC panels with 10″ HMI display real-time flow, pressure, and temperature.
- Mass flow meters, pressure transducers, and differential-pressure switches feed into the station EMS for automated fill sequencing and stock management.
- Emergency-stop buttons and dispenser nozzle interlocks comply with EN ISO 17268 and SAE J2601 standards.

Safety Features

- Integrated hydrogen-leak detectors at each nozzle.
- Over-pressure relief via flame-arrested DN40 vent lines.
- Automatic purge with nitrogen when dispenser is idle or during maintenance.

Standards &amp; Testing

- CE-marked to ATEX 2014/34/EU, PED 2014/68/EU, and EMC 2014/30/EU.
- Factory Acceptance Tests include: flow-rate verification, pre-cooling performance, leak-tightness, emergency shutdown response, and metering accuracy.
